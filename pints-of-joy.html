Pints of Joy —
Inventory Management App

From my first UX case study to a working product a real business uses every day.

Overview

Pints of Joy is an inventory app that helps small businesses order and organize stock without the shortages, missed counts, and last-minute supply runs that plague manual systems. It began as my first end-to-end UX project and grew into a functional web app in real-world use.

The problem. Small-restaurant inventory is plagued by recurring shortages and missed orders, forcing managers to constantly run out for minor essentials.

The goal. Close those gaps so stock flows continuously — no shortages, no scramble.

Phase 1

Designing the concept

This was the first product I ever designed end to end. I leaned on the UX process I was learning — research, personas, wireframes, prototypes, usability testing, and accessibility — and I owned every step.

Understanding the user

I focused on the people doing daily inventory work — mostly restaurant managers and owners — and interviewed them to understand their pain points and whether the process could be automated through an app.

Pain points

  1. Time — counting and ordering inventory is slow, tedious work.
  2. Human error — people forget to count or order items, which disrupts operations.
  3. Not inclusive — most inventory apps target warehouses and big numbers, not small-scale restaurants.
Sugam Pokhrel
Restaurant Manager

Needs to manage inventory efficiently so the restaurant runs smoothly. Goals: manage inventory more efficiently; track stock in and out with less hassle.

"Even with a thorough list, we're human — we forget sometimes."

"My employees don't always coordinate, so we over- or under-order."

Sandra Smith
Senior Manager

Needs to get the right items to every restaurant, or pay for expensive express shipping. Goals: coordinate across branches; track invoices and expenses; build an ordering system every employee can use.

"Coordinating orders across all the restaurants is hard."

"Miscommunication and delays lead to shortages — and that's hectic."

User journey — Sugam (managing inventory)

The task isn't hard; human error makes it hard. Across the flow — checking inventory, making a list, ordering, confirming receipt, and organizing — the opportunities were clear: coordinate with employees, build a complete item list, give easy access to all items, and keep storage well organized.

User journey — Sandra (managing 15 corporate restaurants)

Sending orders to 15 restaurants makes communication the key challenge. The opportunities: a pre-made list, one channel for all incoming orders, an automated system that compares orders against existing inventory, shipment tracking, and easy manager-to-manager communication.

Starting the design

Paper wireframes → digital wireframes → low-fidelity prototype. Core flow: users land on the homepage and scan an item to add it to or subtract it from inventory.

Usability studies

I ran a moderated, in-person study on the low-fi prototype and an unmoderated, in-person study on the high-fi prototype.

Round 1 found: users wanted a clearer flow and a back button; easier item scanning; and an organized area for items and their counts.

Round 2 found: users wanted a tabulated item list; easy access with icons and a back button; and more information on the home screen.

Refining the design

Mockups → high-fidelity prototype → accessibility updates.

Accessibility: added icons and back buttons for easier navigation; used high-contrast colors for visually impaired users; and added item images for quick recognition.

Phase 2

Building it for real

After designing the concept, I partnered with [Company] to turn it into a working product. I built the front end in Vue.js, translating my high-fidelity designs into a live interface their team could actually use on the floor.

What I built: [scan-to-update inventory, searchable item list, low-stock alerts, order list]. I designed a dark theme because the tool is used throughout long shifts — lower glare is easier on the eyes.

Impact: [Company] now uses Pints of Joy as part of daily operations. [Add one real number: tracks X items · cut weekly inventory time from X to Y · X fewer missed orders per month.]

What I learned

As my first UX project, this taught me to design for the way people actually work, not the way a process looks on paper. Putting myself in the users' shoes — and applying Gestalt principles to keep the interface readable at a glance — made the design more intuitive. Building the front end myself taught me where clean design meets real engineering constraints.

Next steps

  1. Another round of usability testing on the scanner screen — it's the heart of the app.
  2. Add features and subtle animation for clearer feedback.
  3. Expand alt text and assistive-tech support.
