Overview

Pints of Joy is an inventory app for small businesses — designed to close the gaps between counts, orders, and shelves, so stock flows continuously without shortages or last-minute supply runs. This page has two parts: the UX case study where I first designed it end to end during my Google UX Design Professional Certificate coursework, and the real product I later built for an actual business, Pints of Joy, that now uses it every day.

Section 01 · UX case study

Inventory App

This was the first product I ever designed end to end — a capstone-style case study for my Google UX Design Professional Certificate. I leaned on the UX process I was learning in real time — research, personas, wireframes, prototypes, usability testing, and accessibility — and owned every step, from the first user interview to the final high-fidelity screen.

Role
Lead UX Designer & Researcher
Timeline
Sep 2024 – Mar 2025
Tools
Figma, Illustrator
Deliverables
Wireframes, prototypes, usability studies

The problem. A persistent challenge in restaurant inventory management is recurring shortages and missed orders, which forces managers to constantly run out for minor essentials.

The goal. Bridge those gaps and keep stock flowing continuously within the restaurant — no shortages, no scramble.

Understanding the user

I focused on the people doing daily inventory work — mostly restaurant managers and owners — and interviewed them to understand their pain points and whether the process could be automated through an app.

Pain points

  1. Time — counting and ordering inventory is slow, tedious work.
  2. Human error — people forget to count or order items, which disrupts operations.
  3. Not inclusive — most inventory apps target warehouses and big numbers, not small-scale restaurants.
Sugam Pokhrel
Restaurant Manager

Needs to manage inventory efficiently so the restaurant runs smoothly. Goals: manage inventory more efficiently; track stock in and out with less hassle.

"Even with a thorough list, we're human — we forget sometimes."

"My employees don't always coordinate, so we over- or under-order."

Sandra Smith
Senior Manager

Needs to get the right items to every restaurant, or pay for expensive express shipping. Goals: coordinate across branches; track invoices and expenses; build an ordering system every employee can use.

"Coordinating orders across all the restaurants is hard."

"Miscommunication and delays lead to shortages — and that's hectic."

User journey — Sugam (managing inventory)

The task isn't hard; human error makes it hard. Across the flow — checking inventory, making a list, ordering, confirming receipt, and organizing — his mood swings from confused and bored, through anticipation and frustration while ordering, to relief and cautious optimism once everything's put away. The opportunities were clear: coordinate with employees, build a complete item list, give easy access to all items, and keep storage well organized.

User journey — Sandra (managing 15 corporate restaurants)

Sending orders to 15 restaurants makes communication the key challenge. Her journey starts relaxed and focused, dips into annoyance and overwhelm as orders come in and get prepared, then eases into relief once every shipment is confirmed. The opportunities: a pre-made list, one channel for all incoming orders, an automated system that compares orders against existing inventory, shipment tracking, and easy manager-to-manager communication.

Starting the design

Paper wireframes → digital wireframes → low-fidelity prototype. Core flow: users land on the homepage and scan an item to add it to or subtract it from inventory.

View the low-fidelity prototype in Figma →

Usability studies

I ran a moderated, in-person study on the low-fi prototype and an unmoderated, in-person study on the high-fi prototype.

Round 1 found: users wanted a clearer flow and a back button; easier item scanning; and an organized area for items and their counts.

Round 2 found: users wanted a tabulated item list; easy access with icons and a back button; and more information on the home screen.

Refining the design

Mockups → high-fidelity prototype → accessibility updates.

View the high-fidelity prototype in Figma →

Accessibility: added icons and back buttons for easier navigation; used high-contrast colors for visually impaired users; and added item images for quick recognition.

Takeaways

As my first UX project, this taught me to design for the way people actually work, not the way a process looks on paper. Putting myself in the users' shoes — and applying Gestalt principles to keep the interface readable at a glance — made the design more intuitive.

Where I left it

  1. Another round of usability testing on the scanner screen — it's the heart of the app.
  2. Consult a backend developer to see how it could work in the real world.
  3. Add features and subtle animation for clearer feedback.
  4. Expand alt text and assistive-tech support.
